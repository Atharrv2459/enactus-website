Startups by students as side ventures

By Enactus MUJ | December 2025

The most powerful student ventures don't usually start with a business plan or a pitch deck. They start with someone looking at something broken in their immediate surroundings and deciding they can't just leave it that way. That restlessness — not genius, not funding, not a perfect idea — is what separates ventures that actually get built from ones that stay on whiteboards. There's a pattern to the student led startups that create real change, and it's worth understanding. They tend to be low tech and high trust. They tend to fail at least once before they find their footing. And they're almost always built around a relationship with a community before they're built around a product or service.

Consider the kind of initiative that emerges when students in a campus town notice that local artisans — people with real, irreplaceable skills — can't afford raw materials at fair prices. Meanwhile, the campus itself is generating tonnes of waste every week that ends up in landfills. The connection between those two facts doesn't require a business degree. It requires someone who's paying attention to both and willing to ask whether they could be linked. That kind of waste to craft model — collecting campus waste, processing it into usable craft materials, and supplying it to artisans — is simple, circular, and it keeps going because it creates value at both ends. Or consider a student who does a rural internship and notices that the people most eligible for government welfare schemes are often the last to know they exist, because the information is buried in English language documents that nobody in the village can easily navigate. Building a simple voice based local language information system — not a sophisticated app, just something that works for the people who need it — and helping 200 families access benefits they were owed but never received is impact. That happened because someone listened before they built anything. The microloan circle model is another example worth thinking about. In communities where daily wage workers can't access formal credit because they have no credit history — and have no credit history because no formal system has ever included them — students have created peer guarantee lending circles that break that cycle. The accounting is simple. The relationships make it work. Default rates are near zero when people are accountable to their neighbours rather than to a bank they've never met. What these ventures share isn't scale or sophistication. They share a commitment to listening before building, to designing things that can be handed off rather than things that collapse when the founders move on, and to accepting that the first version will be wrong. That willingness to be wrong, and to stay in it anyway, is what eventually produces something worth staying in.
